Pacific Northwest Ag Climate Tools

Our mission is to provide management-oriented climate-change decision support systems for Pacific (PNW) agriculture.

Vision

The PNW region's research community has made advances in characterizing the impacts of climate change on agriculture. However, there is a significant gap in translating this into specific management implications that are relevant and useful for risk analysis and decision making. Our vision is to be at the forefront of bridging this gap one step at a time. We believe this is achievable through partnerships with research and extension groups/agencies that are actively involved in current growing season decision support for agriculture, and the users of these tools.

Our current focus: codling moth

As a first step, this website is a means to explore changing codling moth pressures, and associated pest management implications for the Washington State tree fruit industry. Expected changes to bloom timing in multiple apple varieties are also available. This is one part of a broader ongoing collaboration between the Washington State University's (WSU) Center for Sustaining Agriculture and Natural Resources (CSANR) and the WSU Tree Fruit Research and Extension Center (TFREC) to characterize production risks and management implications of a changing climate on Northwest tree fruit production. We have also initiated collaborations with other aspects of Pacific Northwest agriculture. So stay tuned for more this year!

Why the codling moth?

The codling moth is a primary pest affecting tree fruit; its larvae feed on the fruit making it unmarketable. Without management, damage can lead to over 90% fruit loss, making pest management key to a viable and successful regional tree-fruit industry. Given that insect occurrence and growth are primarily temperature-driven, warming is expected to affect their physiology, phenology, and spatial distribution. This will change expected pest pressures, and strategies for pest management.
The growth and management of the codling moth are relatively well understood and translated into a model that is part of the WSU tree fruit Decision Aid System (DAS): a weather-based model used for integrated pest management on more than 90% of Washington State tree-fruit acres. We leverage this knowledge and use it as a starting point to explore what future temperatures might mean. So join us and explore changing codling moth pressures, and associated pest management implications for the Washington State tree fruit industry. You can start with "What's the story?" on the left sidebar to get a quick summary.

Codling Moth Life cycle

The codling moth overwinters as a mature larva in a silk cocoon. In early spring, the overwintering larvae begin pupation, progress to the adult growth stage, mate, and lay eggs which hatch into larvae. In orchards, the larvae find and burrow into the fruit, usually within approximately one day after hatching. The larvae grow and mature in the fruit, coming out to build their cocoon and overwinter or pupate. And the cycle begins again. Each cycle is called a generation of the pest. In Washington State, we typically experience two full generations, and in some warm years a partial third generation of the codling moth.

Within each generation, a certain proportion of larvae pupate and start the next generation cycle. The rest go into a state of delayed development and inactivity (diapause induction). If these diapause-induced larvae reach full growth, they will overwinter successfully. Sometimes, the last generation diapause-induced larvae do not receive enough growing degree day accumulation to grow enough to successfully overwinter.
The fraction of larvae that are diapause-induced versus those that pupate is a function of the photoperiod or day length. When the days are shorter, a larger fraction of the larvae are diapause-induced for overwintering and a smaller fraction pupate into the next generation. When the days are longer, a smaller fraction is diapause-induced for overwintering, and a larger fraction pupates into the next generation.

Field measures at selected tree-fruit growing locations in Washington State helped develop an empirical relationship between day length and the percentage of larvae that were diapause-induced (or conversely, the percent that escaped diapause-induction and pupated). In Washington State, almost 100% of larvae are diapause-induced before mid-April and after late August. Diapause-induced larvae that emerged in August sometimes do not accumulate enough growing degree days to mature enough to overwinter successfully.

     

Codling moth growth is primarily temperature-driven, as is the case with cold-blooded insects in general. Degree day models have been used successfully in forecasting the timing of growth stages, and these models are commonly used to provide integrated pest management decision support such as through the WSU Tree-Fruit Decision Aid System (DAS). Our analyses are based on the degree day models for codling moth developed for and used in DAS.

Codling Moth Management Strategies

Codling moth management aims to disrupt three developmental stages: eggs, newly hatched larvae, and adults. Eggs can be prevented from hatching with topical or residual pesticides. Parasitoid wasps such as Trichogramma have also been shown to help suppress codling moth populations by attacking the egg stage. Newly hatched codling moth larvae can be targeted with residual pesticides or with the codling moth granulosis virus. Applications targeting the newly hatched larvae need to be timed perfectly, because the larvae enter the safety of the fruit within a day of hatching. Finally, mating disruption is directed at the adult codling moths. It delays and prevents mating and, thus, leads to a substantial reduction of eggs laid in the orchard. Management programs combine these strategies in one way or another to keep fruit damage below the economic threshold.
For further details on using the WSU Tree-Fruit Decision Aid System to inform the strategies and timing of codling moth control efforts, please see "What Makes an Effective Moth Management Program?" on the DAS website.








Climate Data

The codling moth tool allows you to explore how codling moth pressure is expected to change in the future, as temperatures warm. It does this by comparing pest risk and key developmental stages of codling moth modeled using historical climate conditions, on the one hand, to those same risk and stages modeled using projected future climate conditions.

Historical Data

The climate data used to model codling moth under historical conditions are gridded meteorological observations based on Abatzoglou (2011), for the period from 1979 to 2016.

Future Climate Projections

The climate data used to model codling moth into the future starts with model output from the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project 5 (CMIP5; Taylor et al., 2012). CMIP5 is meant to provide a framework for coordinated climate change experiments, and is a collaborative effort across multiple research groups running global general circulation models to produce comparable projections of climatic variables into the future. The global future climate projections are too coarse in resolution for many regional applications, and so have been downscaled using the Modified Multivariate Adaptive Constructed Analog (MACA) method (Abatzoglou and Brown, 2012).
In the codling moth tool, the future climate projections are considered in three time frames into the future:

  • 2040s: refers to the range of years 2025 to 2055.
  • 2060s: refers to the range of years 2045 to 2075.
  • 2080s: refers to the range of years 2065 to 2095.
How temperatures are expected to change in the future is dependent on how far, how quickly, and how consistently the greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere accumulate. As no one knows for sure how those concentrations will change, CMIP5 provides global data under different greenhouse gas concentration pathways, known in the climate modeling literature as Representative Concentration Pathways (or RCPs). Each RCP could result from different combinations of economic, technological, demographic, policy, and institutional futures.
The codling moth tool used downscaled climate data developed under two of these representative greenhouse gas concentration pathways:
  • RCP 4.5, the second lowest concentration pathways considered in CMIP5, where the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere is increasing, and expected to stabilize after the year 2100. This is the "lower temperature change" climate change scenario.
  • RCP 8.5, the highest concentration pathway considered in CMIP5, which corresponds to relatively higher greenhouse gas concentrations and temperature increase projections than the RCP 4.5 scenario. This is therefore the "higher temperature change" climate change scenario. Therefore, temperature-based impacts are typically more pronounced for the RCP 8.5 scenario.








Changing Codling Moth Pest Pressures: What's the story?

  1. We expect to see a higher codling moth risk-more number of generations and stronger generations-as temperatures get warmer.
    Historically Washington State has experienced two full generation cycles of the codling moth and a partial third generation in warmer areas. By the 2040s, all of Washington is projected to experience a full third generation each year. Some areas even face a significant fourth generation of codling moth by the 2040s, and this risk becomes more widespread further out into the future. Check out the "Pest risk" tab.
    Management implication
    Increased pest risk implies the need for additional sprays, a larger range of chemicals, and an increased cost of pest control. It also implies increased pesticide resistance risks.
  2. We expect to see all codling moth development stages starting earlier.
    The first flight of the overwintering generation and all subsequent phenological events are projected to begin earlier in the year. The projected advancement is about 8 days on average every 20 years. The shift in the timing of these events also has implications for the relative fraction of larvae that get diapause-induced versus progressing to emerge as adult moths. Check out the "Codling Moth Flight" and "Codling Moth Egg Hatch" tabs.
    Management implication
    Timing of sprays will need to be shifted accordingly. Traps need to be set earlier than typically done in the region, or the ideal window for control measures might be missed.
  3. We expect to see a greater proportion of the first generation overwintering and causing damage the following year. In contrast, a greater proportion of the second generation causes damage in the current year.
    The relative fraction of larvae getting diapause-induced (and therefore overwintering until next season) versus pupating (and progressing to the next generation that same season) is a function of the day length (see codling moth life cycle tab for more details). When days are shorter, a larger fraction of larvae get diapause-induced. Conversely, when days are longer, a smaller fraction of larvae get diapause-induced. With earlier development, the first generation eggs hatch into larvae earlier in the year when the days are still short, so a larger fraction of them get diapause-induced and overwinter, thereby requiring control the following year. On the other hand, earlier development means the second generation eggs hatching into larvae when the days are longer and the relative fraction of larvae escaping diapause and causing further issues in the current year is larger. Check out the "Diapause" tab.
    Management implication
    More control than currently typical might be needed to address the second generation of codling moth.
  4. We expect to see a shorter window of time for effective control, but control mechanisms such as pesticides and mating disruption will work better under warmer climates.
    Warming leads to accelerated degree day accumulation, so that more of the pest population pass through susceptible stages for pest control for a given pesticide residual time, making pesticide treatments more effective. However, the window of opportunity for control is smaller, and even a day's delay can allow significant number of larvae to enter the fruit and cause damage. Similarly, mating delay is a function of degree day accumulation. Faster accumulation implies that a within a given delay, there will be much larger decrease in the population of fertilized eggs.
    Management implication
    The window of opportunity for control is shorter, and it is critical to capture it right. However, if captured correctly, there might be benefits in terms of more effective control.
  5. We expect bloom timing in apple trees to shift to earlier in the year.
    Warming leads to accelerated degree day accumulation in apple trees, and shifts in bloom timing to earlier in the year.
    Management implication
    Stay tuned for details on this!
